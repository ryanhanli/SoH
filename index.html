What is willpower?

“Everything is generated through your own will power.” -Ray Bradbury

  • Willpower is the ability to resist short-term temptations and desires in order to achieve long-term goals

  • Willpower is already present within all of us. Have you ever decided to study when hanging out with friends was more tempting? Willpower.

  • Willpower is essential to thriving in life. If one wants to have a successful experience in college and beyond-in terms of academics, relationships, happiness, and health, willpower is key.

Why is willpower so important?

Empirical research answers this question.

A study done in 1972 by Stanford-based psychologist Walter Mischel showcases the capabilities of willpower. Known as the marshmallow test, Mischel and colleagues presented preschoolers with a plate of marshmallows. Each child was then offered a choice between a small but immediate reward, or two small rewards if they waited for a period of time. The child could eat the one and only marshmallow immediately, or wait for the researcher to leave and come back to have the opportunity to eat two marshmallows.

Years later, the research team followed up with these kids and found that the children who waited for the second marshmallow were generally faring better in life scoring higher on tests, making more money, and being more resiliant and confident.

Willpower Benefits Continued...

Overall, self-control appears to be a better predictor of academic achievement than intelligence. It is also a stronger determinant of effective leadership than charisma and more important for marital satisfaction than empathy.

Anywhere you look at it, people with greater willpower are:

  • -Happier

  • -Healthier

  • -More satisfied in their relationships

  • -Wealthier and further ahead in their careers

  • -More able to manage stress, deal with conflict and overcome adversity

How does willpower work?

We now know why willpower is so important, so let's see how it works.

Let's take a look at another study that was done at Florida State University by Roy Baumesiter and his colleagues. This study involved the use of cookies and radishes. Participants were split into two groups in which one group could eat the cookies while the other could only eat the radishes. Afterwards, the researchers told both groups to do a set of unsolvable math problems. The results? The group who got to eat the cookies lasted more than twice as long trying to solve the puzzles.

What does this mean? Willpower is like a muscle. The more you use it, the weaker it gets, until it's all gone. The radish eaters depleted their willpower resisting cookies, and didn't have as much willpower to stick with the puzzle as the cookie eaters did. Almost everything we do requires willpower and as the day progresses our strength is progressively sapped.

Understanding Willpower

How can we make the most of the our willpower?

  • Willpower Vampires

    When we are depleted of willpower we make bad decisions and are significantly less productive. Beware of these common willpower vampires: making decisions, low blood sugar, sleep deprivation, multitasking, and restraining impulses.

  • Willpower can be Strengthened

    Willpower is like a muscle. The more one exercises it, the stronger it gets. However the strain you put on your willpower is important. If you lift too little weight, there won't be enough strain. Lift too much and you'll overwhelm yourself. Try and set realistic goals that your willpower can handle.

  • Rest

    Just like a muscle, willpower needs to be rested as well. To ensure one doesn't succumb to temptations, one should identify the impulses that tempt them the most and ensure they give their willpower enough rest to deal with them. Thus, try and do productive things earlier in the day when willpower is high.

How can we take action about our willpower?

  • Breath, Move, Sleep

    Fnding yourself falling victim to temptation? Try closing your eyes, taking twenty breaths and focusing on each cycle. Doing this in moments of weakness can help one triumph over temptation. Exercise and sleep also help by optimizing heart rate variability which is a strong indicator of willpower.

  • Out of Sight, Out of Mind

    When we make it difficult for us to accomplish a task, we are less likely to do it. Have trouble staying off your phone during moments of studying? Put the phone in another room.

  • Two Wills Are Better Than One

    Social support can be key in boosting willpower. When we team up with friends, we no longer only hold ourselves accountable for our actions but them as well. Furthermore, studies show that simply observing people exerting their willpower can raise our own levels.

The Takeaway

Be sure to remember

  • Willpower is a key element to happiness in college and life. It affects the qualities of grades, friendships, and health.

  • Willpower is like a muscle. The more one exercises it, the stronger it becomes. Likewise, it needs to be rested and many everyday activities can deplete it.

  • There are many ways we can optimize our willpower such as teaming up with others and cutting our temptations out of our lives.
